Mission 2070

A fun, interactive report about practical climate mitigations society could scale up by 2070 in clean water, renewable energy, and alternative agriculture.

Climate mitigation means lowering the gases that heat the planet, especially carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide. Some solutions are super high-tech. Others are almost suspiciously normal: fix leaks, stop wasting food, protect wetlands, and make electricity cleaner.

Mission briefing

Here is the big idea before you launch into the sections.

By 2070, society could be running many daily systems very differently. The goal is not one magic invention. It is a team of solutions: cleaner electricity, smarter water systems, and farms that feed people while releasing less planet-warming pollution.

The most realistic future mixes three strategies: avoid emissions by replacing fossil fuels, cut waste so fewer resources are needed, and restore natural systems that help store carbon and protect communities. The key word is scale: a technology can exist today, but it only changes the climate when cities, countries, businesses, and people use it widely and fairly.

🧯 Avoid

Use less fossil fuel by switching to solar, wind, geothermal, efficient machines, and electric transportation.

🔁 Reuse

Treat wastewater, recycle nutrients, repair leaks, and stop wasting food before it becomes methane-producing trash.

🌍 Restore

Protect wetlands, rivers, soils, forests, and coastal ecosystems so they store carbon and buffer floods and heat.

Clean Water: the hidden climate hero

Water may not sound like a climate mitigation topic at first. But moving, heating, cleaning, and treating water uses energy. Wastewater can also release methane and nitrous oxide. By 2070, clean water systems could become climate-smart lifelines.

Possible path to 2070

These dates are not predictions. They are a realistic-style roadmap showing how today’s tools could grow if society invests, experiments, and cooperates.

2020s
  • Solar, wind, and batteries get cheaper and spread faster.
  • Cities test water reuse, leak detection, and biogas capture.
  • Food waste and alternative protein solutions become more visible.
2030s
  • More clean electricity powers cars, buildings, pumps, and factories.
  • Water utilities track methane and nitrous oxide more carefully.
  • Farmers get better tools for precise fertilizer and irrigation.
2050s
  • Many grids become mostly zero-carbon, with storage and flexible demand.
  • Wastewater plants act like resource recovery centers.
  • Diet choices, low-methane livestock, and waste cuts shrink food emissions.
2070
  • Clean power supports safe water, resilient farms, and electrified communities.
  • Wetlands, soils, and forests are treated like climate infrastructure.
  • Mitigation is built into everyday design, not treated as an extra.

Myth busters

Open these if you like arguing with oversimplified internet comments.

“Renewable energy cannot work because the sun does not always shine.”

That is a real challenge, but not a deal-breaker. A reliable clean grid can mix solar, wind, geothermal, hydropower, storage, transmission lines, flexible demand, and backup power. The future grid is more like a team than a single superhero.

“Clean water is only about adaptation, not mitigation.”

Clean water is definitely about adaptation, because droughts and floods affect water safety. But it can also mitigate climate change by reducing energy use, capturing methane from wastewater, reusing water, and restoring carbon-storing wetlands.

“Alternative agriculture means everyone must eat weird food.”

Nope. It means more choices and less waste. Some people may choose plant-based or fermentation-made foods. Farmers may use better fertilizer timing, drought-resistant crops, improved animal feed, compost, agroforestry, or precision irrigation. It is a toolkit, not one menu.

“Technology alone will fix everything.”

Technology matters, but rules, fairness, funding, education, land rights, community trust, and smart habits matter too. A solar panel, a wetland, and a farm sensor all work better when people design systems around them.
